Impressive Demos, Unreliable Products

When Google’s AI Leadership Asks for the Wrong Improvements – By Ed Dogan

Over the long weekend, I decided to put two popular AI coding assistants to the test: Google AI Studio Code Assistant and Claude. I gave them the same prompt — build a simple memory game app — and watched how they approached it.

Google AI Studio

Google’s assistant jumped straight into coding without asking a single clarifying question. It built the app in TypeScript, despite my instructions that it would need to be deployed to the Google Play Store.

  • Time Taken: ~2 minutes 11 seconds (including ~35 seconds of debugging)
  • Approach: Prioritized speed above everything else
  • Result: The game ran briefly but crashed after finding the first pair

Claude

Claude, on the other hand, paused to ask four clarifying questions — even asking me what the game should be called. It then built the app with create-react-expo, a framework better suited for mobile deployment.

  • Time Taken: ~7 minutes 30 seconds to build, plus ~4 minutes 40 seconds to run & test (including waiting for my approvals)
  • Approach: Focused on intent alignment and usability
  • Result: The game worked almost perfectly, with only a minor animation bug

The Tradeoff

In real-world engineering, a broken answer delivered instantly is worse than a slower one that actually works.

Google clearly optimized for speed, but the end product was unstable. Claude optimized for accuracy and intent, and the outcome was usable. This raises a bigger question: what are AI leaders asking their teams to optimize for?

If “faster demos” is the only directive, we’ll keep getting impressive showcases that fail in practice. Reliability and alignment should matter just as much as speed.

Takeaway

This experiment reminded me that the way leadership frames success for AI teams has massive downstream effects. Optimizing only for speed might win the demo, but optimizing for accuracy builds tools that engineers can actually trust.

Impressive demos, unreliable products. That’s not the future I want to build.
